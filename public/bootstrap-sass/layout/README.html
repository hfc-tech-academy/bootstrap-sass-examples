Bootstrap-sass on Rails

Layout

Bootstrap provides a number of tools to build out your site, including the grid system, prebuilt components, standardized buttons and alerts, and various mixins that you can use in your SASS. The most commonly used is the grid system for laying out pages.

The grid system (some interesting history here http://www.smashingmagazine.com/2009/07/17/lessons-from-swiss-style-graphic-design/ ) refers to a style of designing pages where you lay out your elements on a horizontal grid, or equally sized horizontal columns. You place the left side of every component at the left side of a column, and the right side of every component on the right side of a column. You can span as many columns as you like, but the left side of all columns will "line up" with the left side of other columns. The span in between columns is called the gutter, so if you have, for example text, in adjacent columns there will be a space between them. Here's the framework of an lg grid (more on that later):

That lays things out horizontally and the height of the component is determined by the amount of content in it. The way that the typography is set up (meaning the font faces, font sizes, margins, padding and line-height of the various elements) will also give you a good vertical visual rythm.

You decide the left-to-right-ness of your design, and the content decides how tall it needs to be. It is a simple way of creating visual harmony on the site.

By default, the grid system of bootstrap has 12 columns, and the gutter width is 30px. These can be changed with the $grid-columns and $grid-gutter-width varibles.

All columns need to be in a row, which in turn needs to be in either a container or a container-fluid. A row specifies a group of columns. The columns get layed out left to right, and they will wrap to the next line if the total is more than 12 (or $grid-columns). Columns will never be split up - never get hyphonated like words might - so you may have empty space where the column doesn't fit, in the same way that word wrapping works in a document.

If you want to introduce white space in a layout, this can be done by using an offset column. This is very much like empty content but responds differently if the browser window is smaller, for example, when people are viewing your site on a mobile browser.

A container or container-fluid defines the overall margins of the site at various sizes. container has a fixed width at each screen size, so if you resize a window you will see the margins snap to different sizes. container-fluid tries to use as much space as possible, so when you change the window size the columns grow and shrink fluidly.

In some sense, you don't know the "actual width" of the columns. There are 12 of them, but depending upon the window size, if it's in a container or container-fluid, the actual size of the columns will vary. The design of the page needs to take this into account.

Numerology Aside

When it comes to measurement units, the USA is a bit of a laughing stock still using some weirdo "Imperial Measurement" system rather than the more rationalized Metric System. The metric system is a steely eyed homage to the phase change of water, a bar of metal in Paris, and the number ten, and is more "Rational". Handy if you care how many millimeters are in a kilometer but, honestly, 10 actually sucks as a number.

Except in the case where you want to easily move a decimal place right or left, or use fingers as a arthimetical device, 10 is not a very useful number. If you have 10 things, and want to share them equally with 2 people, no problem. Share them with 3 people? 4? Not possible, you either have 1 or 2 left over. The prime factors of 10 are 2 and 5, so those are the only way to split it up evenly -- give 2 people 5 things, or 5 people 2 things.

12 -- the number of inches in a foot, or the number of hours in a day -- is a much better number, since it's prime factors are 2, 2, 3. To spell out the options:

  • 2 x 2 x 3 = 12
  • (2 x 2) x 3 = 4 x 3 = 12
  • 2 x (2 x 3) = 2 x 6 = 12

This gives you a lot more options for splitting up things evenly:

  • If you want to split your design in half: 6 columns, and 6 columns.
  • If you want to lay out your page in 3 equal parts: 4 columns, 4 columns, and 4 columns.
  • If you want to lay out your page in 4 equal parts: 3 columns, 3 columns, 3 columns, and 3 columns.

You can't split it evenly into 5 columns. If you multiply 5 x 12 to get a number you can split into five parts you get 60, which you'll notice as the number of minutes in an hour. And that's why you can split an hour into half (30 mins), thirds (20 mins), fourths (15 mins), fifths (12 mins), and sixths (10 mins).

Keep in mind how awesome 12 is if you are itching to redefine the number of columns there are in the bootstrap grid.

The top row has a 2 column logo, 1 column offset, and an 8 column content area. That would like:

<div class="row">
  <div class="col-md-2">
    Logo
  </div>

  <div class="col-md-offset-1 col-md-8 centered">
    <p>Whole bunch of icons</p>
  </div>
<div>

col-md-2 and col-md-8 specific the content, while the col-md-offset-1 is added to the class to shift it over. On smaller screens, this offset won't take effect.

The next area is 2 columns, 8 columns, and 2 columns:

What happens if my column sizes add up to more than 12?

There are two different scenarios:

Things get layed out left to right, and when the number of columns in a <div class="row"> exceed 12, they wrap around. The column doesn't get split, so if there are 3 columns, each col-md-5, the first two will be right to left, there will be a space on the right of 2, and the third column will be below.

In this situation, things get packed in as they can. If you want to force a split between them, use a <div class="clearfix"> which will force everything to be stacked.

The other scenario is when you have a row inside of a col. What? This is a situation where you want to have a seperate grid inside of an existing grid, and you'll find that the math above comes in handy. In this case, each time you create a row you get another "12 columns", but 12 columns inside of the parent column!
